You look up at the night sky and think: "It would be amazing to have a telescope to see the Moon up close, the Andromeda galaxy, the rings of Saturn!" **But you don't know anything about astronomy, and you're afraid of buying something complicated that will end up gathering dust in the garage.**

## Why 80% of telescopes end up in the garage

Before we get into specific models:

**Most people who buy a telescope use it 2 or 3 times, then it ends up in the garage.**

Why? Not because the sky isn't interesting. It's because the telescope is:
- Too heavy to bring outside
- Too complicated to set up
- Too bulky to store
- Too frustrating to use

**The result?** You tell yourself "I'll take it out this weekend," and you never do.

So before comparing models, here's what actually matters:

### 1. **Will you actually take it outside?**
A telescope that weighs 10kg and needs 20 minutes to set up? You won't take it out. Period.

### 2. **Is it simple to use?**
If you need to read a 50-page manual to understand how it works, you'll give up.

### 3. **Where does it live when you're not using it?**
If it takes up all the space in your living room or garage, it's going to get on your nerves fast.

### 4. **Does it zoom in enough to see something cool?**
Yes, because the whole point is to see the Moon in detail and the rings of Saturn.

### 5. **Do you understand how it works?**
Or is it a mysterious "black box" you're afraid to touch?

**That's it.** The rest (technical jargon, specs, etc.) doesn't matter for now.

## A real comparison: three telescopes that show you the same thing

We'll compare three telescopes that show exactly the same thing (same magnifying power). The difference? **You'll actually use one of them; the others will end up in the garage.**

### The Flextube Heritage 150P: the "classic" telescope

<img src="/assets/flextube-heritage.jpg" alt="Skywatcher Flextube Heritage telescope">

This is a very popular model, recommended everywhere on forums. It has a good reputation and isn't expensive (~€300-400).

**But here's what nobody tells you:**

#### Problem #1: It's heavy and bulky
- **7 kilos** (imagine carrying it on a bike or a hike)
- **37cm wide x 41cm tall**, even when "folded"
- Hard to fit in a backpack
- Hard to bring on vacation (unless you have a huge trunk)
- You'll need a big special carrying bag (~€50)

#### Problem #2: The focuser is bad

This is **the #1 complaint** from people who own this telescope.

**The problem:**
- The eyepiece holder is **low quality**
- It has **play** (it wobbles in every direction)
- Impossible to get a **precise** focus, especially at high magnification
- If you add a slightly heavy accessory (a good eyepiece), it gets even worse

**In practice:** You spend 5 minutes trying to get a sharp image, and the moment you touch the telescope, everything shifts. Frustrating.

On forums, people say: "the focuser is junk." Many people rig up special tape to try to improve it.

#### Problem #3: You have to adjust it (collimation) constantly

Because of the **sliding tubes** that fold up, the mirrors go out of alignment.

**The problem:**
- You have to **check the mirror alignment** very often
- If it's misaligned, the image is blurry
- For a beginner who doesn't know how to fix it, this is a nightmare

**In practice:** You take out your telescope, and surprise: the image is blurry. You have to adjust everything before observing. You're already fed up.

#### Problem #4: You don't understand how it works

The telescope arrives already assembled in a tube. You can't see what's inside. You don't understand why it works.

**Result:**
- If something goes out of adjustment, you don't know what to do
- You're afraid to break it
- You don't dare customize it
- It's a mysterious "black box"

**Real final price: €300 + €100 tripod + €50 bag = €450**

**Plus a bonus: hours of frustration with focusing and adjustments.**

### The Bresser N 150/750 Messier Hexafoc EXOS-1: the "serious-looking" telescope

<img src="/assets/bresser-messier.jpg" alt="Bresser Messier telescope with equatorial mount">

This is the telescope you see on forums, with people saying "now that's a real telescope." It has a big mount that looks professional (~€400-500).

**But here's the reality:**

#### Problem #1: It's even heavier and bulkier

- **Weight: ~10-12 kilos** with the mount (impossible on a bike or a hike!)
- **Requires a huge tripod** with lots of screws and adjustments
- **Impossible to transport** without a car and plenty of space
- **Takes up a massive amount of room** in the garage (tube + giant tripod)

**In practice:** It's so heavy that you NEVER take it out. It stays in the garage.

#### Problem #2: The "equatorial mount" (= hell for beginners)

You're told it's great because it "tracks the stars automatically." **But nobody mentions that you have to:**

1. **Find Polaris** (good luck if you don't already know it)
2. **Do the "polar alignment"** (point the tripod precisely north)
3. **Set the angle** based on your latitude (do you know your latitude?)
4. **Balance the tube** with counterweights (otherwise it tips over)
5. **Understand the two axes of rotation** (right ascension + declination = a headache)

**The result?** You spend 30 minutes setting the thing up before you even see anything. And if you get the settings wrong, it doesn't work.

**In practice:** It's so complicated that you give up after 2 attempts.

#### Problem #3: It's fragile and expensive

- The mount goes out of adjustment easily
- If you tighten too hard, you break parts
- Spare parts are expensive
- You're afraid to break it every time you handle it

**Price: ~€500 (and you'll never use it)**

## What about the other 150mm telescopes on the market?

You might come across other models while searching. Here's a quick rundown of popular 150mm Newtonian telescopes:

### Telescopes with an equatorial mount (avoid for beginners)
- **Skywatcher N 150/750 PDS Explorer EQ3-2**: Same issue as the Bresser, complicated EQ mount
- **Omegon N 150/750 EQ-4**: Same, too technical for beginners
- **Celestron AstroMaster 130EQ**: Big mount, heavy, complicated
- **GSO N 150/750 Deluxe**: Good optics but EQ mount = too complex
- **TS-Optics N 150/750 Starscope**: Same, equatorial mount not suited to beginners

**Verdict:** All these models are made for people who already have experience. Not for you.

### "Classic" Dobsonian telescopes (large tubes)
- **Skywatcher N 150/1200 Dobson**: Bigger (1.2m tall), impossible to transport
- **Omegon Advanced N 150/750 Dobson**: Big rigid tube, not collapsible, takes up space
- **GSO Dobson N 150/750**: Same, big box, not transportable
- **Orion SkyQuest XT6**: Good telescope, but huge and heavy
- **Levenhuk Ra 150N Dobson**: Same bulk issue

**Verdict:** Good optics, simple to use, BUT too bulky. They stay in your garage.

### The only other "compact" Dobsonian
- **Skywatcher Heritage 150P (FlexTube)**: This is the one we compared above. The cheapest, but the flaws remain (heavy, uncomfortable position).

**Verdict:** OK if your budget is very tight and you only observe at home. Otherwise, you'll regret it.

### Why are we only comparing 150mm telescopes?

Because it's the **ideal aperture for beginners**:
- **114mm or 130mm**: a bit limited, you'll see less planetary detail
- **150mm**: the perfect sweet spot (you really see cool stuff without it being too heavy)
- **200mm and up**: too heavy and bulky for a beginner (and much more expensive)

**All the telescopes listed above have similar optical capabilities.** What really changes is day-to-day practicality.
