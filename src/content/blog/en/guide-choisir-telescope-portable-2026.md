# How to Choose a Portable Telescope in 2026: The Complete Guide

Nomadic astronomy has grown considerably in recent years. Whether you're a passionate amateur astronomer or a traveler curious about the starry sky, **choosing the right portable telescope** is crucial to making the most of your observations on the go.

## Why choose a portable telescope?

### The benefits of astronomical mobility

A **portable telescope** offers unmatched freedom:

- **Escape light pollution**: Bring your instrument to preserved observing sites
- **Astronomy while traveling**: Observe skies around the world during your trips
- **Save space**: Ideal for apartments or small living spaces
- **Spontaneity**: Head out and observe as soon as the sky clears

### Who needs a travel telescope?

- Astronomers living in cities, looking for dark skies
- Travelers wanting to observe under different latitudes
- Hikers and campers who love astronomy
- Families wanting to share astronomy on vacation

## Essential criteria for choosing your portable telescope

### 1. Weight and dimensions

**Criterion #1** for a travel telescope is how easy it is to transport.

**Recommended weight**:
- Light: under 3kg (ideal for hiking)
- Medium: 3 to 6kg (good compromise)
- Heavy: over 6kg (limiting for frequent transport)

**Transport dimensions**:
- Check if the telescope fits in an **airplane cabin** (55×40×20cm)
- Favor models that are **easy to disassemble**
- Consider the volume it takes up in your car

**Our recommendation**: The [Smallest](/product/smallest) weighs 4kg with optics and fits in a plane's cabin (25×25×20cm folded).

### 2. Aperture and optical performance

Don't sacrifice too much performance for portability!

**Common apertures**:
- **70-90mm**: Lunar and planetary observing, very portable
- **114-127mm**: Good performance/portability compromise
- **150mm+**: High performance, requires optimized design

**Focal ratio (F/D)**:
- F/4 to F/5: Ideal for deep sky, compact
- F/6 to F/8: Versatile for planetary and deep sky
- F/10+: Specialized for planetary, bulkier

### 3. Telescope type

#### Newtonian (Reflector)
**Advantages**:
- Excellent value for money
- Good aperture for contained weight
- No chromatic aberration

**Drawbacks**:
- Requires collimation
- Generally longer

**Example**: 150/750 Newtonian (F/5) = 87cm long but collapsible

#### Refractor
**Advantages**:
- No maintenance
- High-contrast image
- Compact at small apertures

**Drawbacks**:
- Expensive at large apertures
- Chromatic aberration (except APO models)

#### Schmidt-Cassegrain / Maksutov
**Advantages**:
- Very compact (short tube)
- Versatile

**Drawbacks**:
- Expensive
- Long cool-down time
- Heavy at large apertures

### 4. The mount

For portability, favor:

**Alt-azimuth mount**:
- Simpler and lighter
- Quick setup
- Sufficient for visual observing

**Dobsonian**:
- Excellent for a portable Newtonian
- Very stable
- Budget-friendly

**Our solution**: The Smallest uses an ultra-compact, built-in Dobsonian base, with no tools needed for setup.

### 5. Setup time

An **often overlooked but essential** criterion!

- Under 5 minutes: Excellent
- 5-10 minutes: Acceptable
- Over 10 minutes: Discouraging for frequent use

A complicated setup discourages spontaneous observing sessions.

## Essential accessories for nomadic astronomy

1. **Suitable carrying bag** (padded, weatherproof)
2. **Red headlamp** (preserves night vision)
3. **Mobile app** (sky maps: Stellarium, SkySafari)
4. **Compact eyepieces** (avoid heavy, bulky Plössls)
5. **Cheshire or collimation laser** (for Newtonians)

## Maintenance tips while traveling

- **Protect the optics** with caps
- **Avoid impacts** (padded bag mandatory)
- **Check collimation** after every transport
- **Clean gently** (blower + microfiber)
- **Store somewhere dry** to avoid mold
